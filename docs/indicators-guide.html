The 6 Indicators — Detailed Breakdown

Indicator 1: EMA Crossover (5/13)

What It Is

Two Exponential Moving Averages — a fast one (5-period) and a slow one (13-period). When the fast EMA crosses above the slow EMA, it signals bullish momentum. When it crosses below, bearish.

The Formula

EMA = Price_today x k + EMA_yesterday x (1 - k) where k = 2 / (period + 1) EMA 5: k = 2/6 = 0.333 (very responsive) EMA 13: k = 2/14 = 0.143 (smoother) Signal: EMA5 > EMA13 → BULL (fast above slow = upward momentum) EMA5 < EMA13 → BEAR (fast below slow = downward momentum)

Why 5/13 Instead of 9/21?

Traditional 9/21 EMA was too slow. By the time it detected a bearish crossover, the move was already 70% done. The 5/13 catches reversals 3-5 candles earlier, which on 5-min data means 15-25 minutes of lead time.

Indicator 2: Price vs EMA 20

What It Is

Simply checks if the current price is above or below the 20-period EMA. This tells you if the market is trading above or below its recent average — a basic but powerful trend filter.

EMA 20 = 20-period Exponential Moving Average Signal: Current Price > EMA 20 → BULL Current Price < EMA 20 → BEAR No NEUTRAL state — it's always one or the other.

Why EMA 20 Instead of EMA 50?

EMA 50 was the original choice but it lagged catastrophically. On real NIFTY data, the EMA 50 took 2-3 hours to flip bearish during a selloff. By then RSI was at 15 and the move was over. EMA 20 responds in ~40 minutes — fast enough to catch intraday reversals while still filtering noise.

Indicator 3: RSI (14-period)

What It Is

Relative Strength Index — measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes on a 0-100 scale. Tells you if the market has been buying (high RSI) or selling (low RSI) recently.

Step 1: Calculate price changes gain = max(change, 0) loss = abs(min(change, 0)) Step 2: Average gains and losses (14-period EMA) avg_gain = EMA(gains, 14) avg_loss = EMA(losses, 14) Step 3: Relative Strength RS = avg_gain / avg_loss Step 4: RSI RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS)) Range: 0 to 100 RSI > 70: Overbought (too much buying) RSI < 30: Oversold (too much selling) RSI 45-55: No clear direction

Indicator 4: MACD (12/26/9)

What It Is

Moving Average Convergence Divergence — measures the relationship between two EMAs. The histogram shows whether momentum is accelerating or decelerating.

MACD Line = EMA(12) - EMA(26) Signal Line = EMA(MACD Line, 9) Histogram = MACD Line - Signal Line Signal: Histogram > 0 → BULL (momentum accelerating up) Histogram < 0 → BEAR (momentum accelerating down) Key moments: Histogram flips + to - : Momentum shifting bearish Histogram flips - to + : Momentum shifting bullish Histogram growing : Trend strengthening Histogram shrinking : Trend weakening

Why It Matters

MACD is excellent at detecting momentum shifts. When the histogram flips from negative to positive, it often marks the START of a move, not the end. This makes it complementary to EMA crossover (which confirms) and RSI (which measures extremes).

Indicator 5: Supertrend (7, 2.5)

What It Is

A volatility-based trend following indicator that plots a single line above (bearish) or below (bullish) the price. Built on ATR (Average True Range) — it adapts to current market volatility.

Step 1: ATR = Average True Range (7 periods) True Range = max(High-Low, |High-PrevClose|, |Low-PrevClose|) ATR = SMA(True Range, 7) Step 2: Bands Upper Band = (High + Low)/2 + 2.5 x ATR Lower Band = (High + Low)/2 - 2.5 x ATR Step 3: Direction Price closes above Upper Band → Flip to BULL (line = lower band) Price closes below Lower Band → Flip to BEAR (line = upper band) Signal: Direction = 1 (price above line) → BULL Direction = -1 (price below line) → BEAR

Why Period=7, Multiplier=2.5?

Default Supertrend uses period=10, multiplier=3.0. We use faster settings (7, 2.5) because:

  • Period 7 = reacts faster to volatility changes (3 candles earlier)
  • Multiplier 2.5 = narrower bands = more sensitive to trend changes
  • On intraday 5-min data, the default was too slow to catch reversals

Indicator 6: Price Action (3-Bar Slope)

What It Is

The simplest indicator — are the last 3 closing prices going up or down? This is the only real-time, non-lagging indicator in the system. All others use historical averages.

last_3_closes = [Close[-3], Close[-2], Close[-1]] slope = (Close[-1] - Close[-3]) / Close[-3] x 100 Signal: slope > +0.02% → BULL (price rising in last 3 candles) slope < -0.02% → BEAR (price falling in last 3 candles) otherwise → NEUTRAL (flat, no direction) On 5-min data: 3 candles = 15 minutes of price movement

Why This Is The Most Important Indicator

Every other indicator tells you what the trend WAS. Price Action tells you what the trend IS right now. On real data analysis:

  • Every losing trade had Price Action as NEUTRAL or opposing
  • Adding a strict gate (Price Action MUST agree) cut losses from 20 to 17
  • Max drawdown dropped 42% (-11,424 to -6,597)
This is our strongest gate. If EMA, RSI, MACD, Supertrend all say BULL but the last 3 candles are falling — the trend is over. Do not enter. This single rule has the highest impact on real data performance.

Additional Indicators (Used for SL/Target, Not Voting)

ATR — Average True Range

Measures market volatility. Used to calculate dynamic stop-loss distance.

True Range = max( High - Low, |High - Previous Close|, |Low - Previous Close| ) ATR(14) = 14-period SMA of True Range Usage: SL = Entry - (ATR x multiplier x delta) NIFTY multiplier: 1.5 BANKNIFTY multiplier: 3.0

Higher ATR = more volatile market = wider SL needed. ATR adapts automatically — in calm markets the SL tightens, in volatile markets it widens.

Open Interest (OI)

Total outstanding option contracts. Used by the OI Reversal strategy and scoring engine. Not a voting indicator but critical for trade selection.

High Put OI at strike → Support level (Writers defend, price won't fall below) High Call OI at strike → Resistance level (Writers defend, price won't rise above) Scoring: OI > 1M = 20 pts OI > 500K = 15 pts OI > 100K = 10 pts OI > 50K = 5 pts OI Reversal strategy: 47% WR, dominant P&L contributor across 9 months of backtesting.

Lowering the bear threshold to 55% lets us catch bearish trends while there is still room to profit from PE options.
